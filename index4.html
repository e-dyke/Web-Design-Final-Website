ARTIST BIO

Elaina will receive her Bachelor of Arts in Art from Columbia College on May 2, 2026. She is a multifaceted artist that works in painting, ceramics, and jewelry. Her work primarily reflects nature. She is a member of the Honors Program at Columbia College, and has previously been a member of the Hannibal Arts Council, and the National Art Honors Society. Her works have been exhibited at the Hannibal Arts Council, Quincy Art Center, Columbia College Sydney Larson Gallery, and numerous other universities across Missouri. She will continue to practice in Missouri via freelance artistry after graduation.

MY STORY

From the time I could hold a crayon, I was drawing. Some of my favorite childhood memories are from long summer days spent in the driveway with my mom, covering the pavement in sidewalk chalk. We would sit together with coloring books while Disney movies played in the background, completely absorbed in creating. I’ve always had a vivid imagination—inventing stories, going on pretend adventures, and crafting something new. At the time, it was simply fun. I had no idea that art would become a constant thread throughout my life.

In elementary and middle school, I was the student who went above and beyond on every project involving creativity—posters, presentations, anything that required design and imagination. I poured myself into each assignment. Coming from a family of artists, I was surrounded by creativity, and from a young age I began taking watercolor lessons from my aunt Nancy and cousin Kelly, which only deepened my love for art. By eighth grade, I had a clear realization: this wasn’t just a hobby—it was a passion. My teachers noticed it too. One of them encouraged me to pursue art in high school, telling me she saw real potential in me. Hearing that from someone outside my family—someone without bias—meant everything. It gave me a new sense of confidence in my abilities.

In high school, I fully committed to developing my skills, taking at least two art classes each semester. I learned foundational techniques—proportion, composition, and how to work across different mediums. In my early high school career, I developed my skills in graphite and charcoal drawing, colored pencil, and acrylic painting. Then, in my junior year, I took my first sculpture class—and everything changed. I was completely captivated by the process. What began with simple pinch pots evolved into more complex forms: mugs, lidded jars, teapots, and even sculptural animal heads. I discovered that I not only loved two-dimensional work, but thrived in three dimensions as well.

By my senior year, I was exploring a wide range of mediums, including sculpture, painting, drawing, and even digital design. While I appreciated learning about Adobe programs, I quickly realized that graphic design wasn’t where my passion lay. I was drawn to tangible materials—the feeling of working with my hands, shaping something real.

As graduation approached, the inevitable question arose: “What are you going to do with your life?” I didn’t have a clear answer. I knew I loved art deeply, but I wasn’t sure how to turn that into a career. It felt uncertain, even intimidating. However, through conversations with my teachers and guidance counselors, I began to see the possibilities. I learned about the many directions I could take—teaching, professorship, freelance work, or even starting my own business. Slowly, my doubts began to fade. The idea of building a life centered around creativity—creating not only for myself, but for others—felt both exciting and meaningful. I decided to pursue it wholeheartedly.

I applied to three colleges in Missouri with strong art programs, and ultimately I chose Columbia College. It was the perfect fit, and thanks to dual credit coursework, I was able to complete my Bachelor of Arts in just three years. During my freshman year, I took my first ceramics class and was immediately captivated. Transforming a simple ball of clay on the pottery wheel into a functional piece was fascinating to me. I learned to create forms such as mugs, pots, and small dishes, while also being taught the technical processes of trimming, firing, and glazing.

The following year, I discovered another passion, jewelry making. I learned how to cut metal with a jeweler’s saw, solder, create surface textures, set stones, and size rings. I quickly fell in love with the precision and craftsmanship it required. Over the next two years, I continued to refine my skills across ceramics, jewelry, and painting. In ceramics especially, I began experimenting with glazing techniques and developed a love for using underglazes to paint on my pieces, treating them like canvases.

At that point, I found myself facing a new question: how could I choose just one medium? But eventually, I realized—I didn’t have to. I’ve always been a multifaceted artist, and I didn’t want to limit myself to a single form of expression. That realization led me to a clear path, working for myself. I want to continue creating what brings me joy, exploring multiple mediums, and sharing that joy with others.

My goal is to build a business rooted in authenticity—creating art that is true to who I am, for as long as I can. This is only the beginning of my journey, and I’m incredibly excited for what the future holds. Thank you for being a part of the start of my story.
